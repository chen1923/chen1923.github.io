Zexu Chen

I build physics-grounded and data-driven models of how NiTi-based metals remember their shape — coupling phase-field theory, GPU-accelerated computing, and Gaussian-process surrogates for inverse alloy design. / Columbus, OH

I'm a PhD candidate in the Wang group at The Ohio State University (expected May 2028), working on phase-field modeling of NiTi-based shape memory alloys.

My research spans three coupled layers: 3D GPU-accelerated multi-variant Allen–Cahn / Cahn–Hilliard solvers in C / OpenACC on the Ohio Supercomputer Center; microstructural engineering of NiTi via 1D nanoscale concentration modulations and Ni₄Ti₃ precipitation; and Gaussian Process Regression surrogates that map composition and microstructure to transformation temperature and stress–strain response — closing the loop for inverse alloy design.

Projects

01 / Lead — In Preparation

Regulating Mechanical Behavior of NiTi SMA via 1D Nanoscale Concentration Modulations

3D phase-field study of how nanoscale composition waves influence the superelastic stress–strain response of NiTi — with relevance to biomedical and actuator applications. Habit-plane and twinning-plane formation in B19′ martensite is analyzed through martensite crystallography and Khachaturyan microelasticity. A Gaussian Process Regression surrogate links modulation parameters to mechanical response to support accelerated inverse design.

Phase-field C · OpenACC Khachaturyan microelasticity GPR surrogate Inverse design
02 / Published 2026

Soft & Hard Confinement Effects on Martensitic Transformation in NiTi

Phase-field study of how amorphous-matrix (soft) and crystalline-matrix (hard) confinement modulate the B2 → B19′ transformation in NiTi shape memory alloys. Demonstrated linear superelastic response routes via amorphous–crystalline composite microstructures. Published in Shape Memory and Superelasticity (2026) with Sriram, Feng & Wang.

Multi-phase field B2 → B19′ Composite microstructure
03

Taming Martensitic Transformation via Dissolution of Ni₄Ti₃ Precipitates

Coupled Cahn–Hilliard and time-dependent Ginzburg–Landau evolution with an explicit nucleation algorithm to model Ni₄Ti₃ precipitation and its dissolution-induced concentration field. Explores the effect on transformation hysteresis and mechanical stability in collaboration with experimentalists. Implemented in C with OpenACC and run as large parametric SLURM campaigns on OSC.

Ni₄Ti₃ Cahn–Hilliard + TDGL GPU / OpenACC SLURM
04

Predicting Superconducting λ and Tc from Eliashberg Spectra

XGBoost and Gaussian Process Regression pipeline predicting λ and Tc from α²F(ω) Eliashberg spectral functions on the Gibson et al. (2026, npj Comput. Mater.) dataset of 806 DFT-validated superconductors. Physics-informed frequency-weighted α²F/ω features aligned with the Eliashberg integral; Matérn (ν = 1.5, 2.5) + White kernels tuned for spectral discontinuities. Benchmarked 50 / 200 / 800-bin resolution against generalization, identifying physics-informed binning as a more robust route than naïve high-resolution sampling.

XGBoost · GPR Matérn kernels Eliashberg α²F Feature engineering
05 / Concept — In Progress

Agentic AI & MCP for Semantic Microstructure Analysis

A researcher-in-the-loop pipeline where a multimodal LLM agent, connected through a Model Context Protocol (MCP) server, turns high-level semantic goals (e.g., “find voids under 1 nm”) into concrete operations — object segmentation, feature extraction, and proximity filtering — over simulation and CT microstructure data. The agent plans, translates intent into logical rules and parameters, validates results against the original goal, and iteratively refines, returning visual reports and segmentation maps.

MLLM agent MCP Microstructure analysis Segmentation Human-in-the-loop
