Zexu Chen

I build physics-grounded and data-driven models of how NiTi-based metals remember their shape — coupling phase-field theory, GPU-accelerated computing, and Gaussian-process surrogates for inverse alloy design. / Columbus, OH

I'm a PhD candidate in the Wang group at The Ohio State University (expected May 2028), working on phase-field modeling of NiTi-based shape memory alloys.

My research spans three coupled layers: GPU-accelerated phase-field solvers written in C / OpenACC and run on the Ohio Supercomputer Center; microstructural engineering of NiTi at the nanoscale; and machine-learning surrogates that link composition and microstructure to mechanical response — closing the loop for inverse alloy design.

Projects

01 / Published 2026

Soft & Hard Confinement Effects on Martensitic Transformation in NiTi

Phase-field study of how amorphous-matrix (soft) and crystalline-matrix (hard) confinement modulate the B2 → B19′ transformation in NiTi shape memory alloys. Demonstrated linear superelastic response routes via amorphous–crystalline composite microstructures. Published in Shape Memory and Superelasticity (2026) with Sriram, Feng & Wang.

Multi-phase field B2 → B19′ Composite microstructure
02

Taming Martensitic Transformation via Precipitate Engineering

Coupled diffusional and non-diffusional phase-field evolution with an explicit nucleation algorithm, used to model precipitation in NiTi and the concentration field it leaves behind. Explores the resulting effect on transformation behavior and mechanical stability. Implemented in C with OpenACC and run as large parametric SLURM campaigns on OSC.

Precipitation Phase-field GPU / OpenACC SLURM
03

Predicting Superconducting λ and Tc from Eliashberg Spectra

XGBoost and Gaussian Process Regression pipeline predicting λ and Tc from α²F(ω) Eliashberg spectral functions, trained on a public dataset of DFT-validated superconductors. Physics-informed, frequency-weighted spectral features are aligned with the Eliashberg integral, with kernels chosen to handle spectral discontinuities. Benchmarks weigh feature resolution against generalization.

XGBoost · GPR Kernel design Eliashberg α²F Feature engineering
04 / Concept — In Progress

Agentic AI & MCP for Semantic Microstructure Analysis

A researcher-in-the-loop pipeline where a multimodal LLM agent, connected through a Model Context Protocol (MCP) server, turns high-level semantic goals into concrete operations — segmentation, feature extraction, and filtering — over simulation and microscopy data. The agent plans, translates intent into logical rules and parameters, validates results against the original goal, and iteratively refines, returning visual reports and segmentation maps.

MLLM agent MCP Microstructure analysis Segmentation Human-in-the-loop
